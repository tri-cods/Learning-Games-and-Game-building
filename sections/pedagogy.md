## What is a good use case for a game?

Think about your learning goals. At the end of the day, a (traditional) video game is a series of computer-based simulations, in which players can explore a virtual environment without real-life consequences. Not every lesson is suited to a video game adaptation, but ones concerning easily modeled/simulated/predictable concepts such as economics, politics, wars, meteorological events, migrations, and scientific experiments are the subject of many games used for education. (Some examples: the widely popular Civilization series, Monopoly.)

So think: 

- Would this topic benefit from playing around in a sandbox environment? 
- Would a sense of freedom and open-ended exploration within this environment or topic encourage better criticism? 
- Would constructing a branching digital narrative help students explore the nuance of a given topic? 

As we delve into game design features in the next section, keep in mind that the same principles apply to physical and LARPing games, including role-playing scenarios. Although we focus on digital games in this workshop, these other types of games are also useful to encourage exploration and engagement in the classroom.

Later we will have a practical portion showing you how to develop games, should you want to create one for students or guide students in creating their own. However, we will unpack principles of game design in the following section, which will provide you with a toolkit to evaluate existing video games to see if they are fitting assignments.

### A note on accuracy

One common fallacy regarding games in the classroom is that they must be factually infallible to have pedagogical value. Video games are cultural artifacts just as films, television and visual art are. Like these other mediums, we want to highlight video games’ usefulness for encouraging critique. When used in tandem with other traditional media such as academic papers or textbooks, games can invite questions such as “Why might a game developer choose to use this incorrect information or stereotype?” or “What common misconceptions of this topic does the game highlight?” How the game is made offers valuable insight into popular perception of its subject matter.
